# Module 01 — Systems Basics

**Phase 1 · Foundations**

Now that you're convinced a system is more than its parts, this module names the parts —
and, more importantly, the thing that binds them. Every system, from a thermostat to a
software organization, has exactly three things going on. Learn to spot them and you have a
lens you can point at anything.

## Objectives

By the end of this module you should be able to:

1. Identify the three components of any system — **elements**, **interconnections**, and a
   **function or purpose** — and explain why the last one is the hardest to see.
2. Explain why changing *elements* alters a system's behavior the least, changing
   *interconnections* alters it more, and changing *purpose* alters it most.
3. Deduce a system's real purpose from its **behavior**, not its stated goals.
4. Draw a defensible **boundary** around a system and explain what your choice includes,
   excludes, and distorts.

## Key concepts

- **Elements.** The parts you can usually see and name — the servers, the people, the
  queues, the trees in a forest. Elements are often the easiest to notice and, surprisingly,
  the least important to a system's behavior. Swap one engineer for another, one router for
  another, and the system mostly keeps doing what it did.
- **Interconnections.** The relationships that hold elements together — the flows of
  information, signals, money, code, and rules that let one element affect another. Change
  these and behavior shifts noticeably. A new escalation policy or a rewired data flow can
  transform a system whose parts never changed.
- **Function or purpose.** The least obvious and most crucial element. Meadows uses
  *function* for non-human systems and *purpose* for human ones. Purpose is deduced from
  **behavior over time**, not from stated goals — "watch what it does, not what it says." A
  team that *says* it values quality but always ships on the deadline has a purpose:
  shipping on the deadline.
- **The leverage ranking.** Changing elements changes a system least; changing
  interconnections changes it more; changing the purpose changes it most. This is a preview
  of the leverage-points work later in the curriculum — purpose sits near the top.
- **Boundaries are inventions.** There is no "correct" boundary. You draw one to make a
  problem tractable, and that choice determines what you can and can't explain. Draw it too
  narrowly and the cause of your problem sits just outside the frame.
