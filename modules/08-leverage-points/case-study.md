# Module 08 — Case Study

> **Your work.** Take a real problem you've tried (or watched someone try) to fix, and find a
> higher-leverage intervention than the obvious one. The goal isn't to be clever — it's to
> practice climbing the ladder past the first parameter you'd have reached for.

## The problem

_Name a real, ongoing problem in a system you know. State it as a behavior you want to change._

## The obvious intervention

_What's the first fix people reach for here? Which leverage point does it act on (probably a
number or a buffer)?_

## Climbing the ladder

_Walk up Meadows' list. For this problem, what would a change to a **delay**, a **loop**, an
**information flow**, a **rule**, and the **goal** each look like? You don't need all twelve — get
to at least three distinct levels._

## The higher-leverage move

_Pick the intervention you think is genuinely higher-leverage than the obvious one. Explain why
it reshapes more of the system._

## Why it's hard

_Higher leverage usually costs more political or organizational capital. What makes this one hard
to actually do — and who would have to act?_

## What I noticed

_Two or three sentences: what did ranking the interventions by leverage change about how you see
this problem?_
