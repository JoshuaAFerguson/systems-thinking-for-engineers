# Module 05 — System Properties

**Phase 1 · Foundations**

Why do some systems keep working through shocks that should have wrecked them? Meadows names
three properties that make systems robust: **resilience, self-organization, and hierarchy.**
This module is about those properties — and about the quiet trade you make every time you
optimize one part at the expense of the whole.

## Objectives

By the end of this module you should be able to:

1. Define **resilience** as the ability to recover and keep functioning under disturbance —
   and explain why it is *not* the same as being efficient, stable, or constant.
2. Explain **self-organization** (a system's capacity to grow, learn, and restructure itself)
   and **hierarchy** (nested subsystems) as sources of robustness.
3. Articulate the **resilience-vs-efficiency trade-off** and recognize where slack, buffers,
   and redundancy were stripped out in the name of optimization.
4. Spot **sub-optimization** — a part optimizing its own goal at the expense of the whole —
   and explain how hierarchy can both reduce information load and fragment a system.

## Key concepts

- **Resilience.** The ability to bounce back — to restore function after being knocked around
  — thanks to loops that can repair, reroute, or rebuild. Resilience is dynamic, not static:
  a resilient system may fluctuate a lot while never losing its footing. Meadows warns that we
  systematically sacrifice resilience for the visible short-term win of efficiency and
  stability, right up until the system loses its ability to recover.
- **Self-organization.** The most powerful form of resilience: a system that can *change
  itself* — learn, diversify, evolve new structure. Self-organizing systems are messy and
  unpredictable, which is exactly why we're tempted to stamp the capacity out in favor of
  control. Doing so makes them brittle.
- **Hierarchy.** Systems nest — subsystems within systems within systems. Hierarchy is how
  complexity stays manageable: it reduces the information any one part must track by keeping
  most interactions *local* to a subsystem. It arises to serve the subsystems and the whole
  together.
- **The efficiency/resilience trade-off.** Redundancy, buffers, and slack look like waste to
  an efficiency drive — a spare server, a cross-trained teammate, inventory just in case. Cut
  them all and you get a lean system with no give, one that shatters at the first disturbance
  it wasn't tuned for. Every optimization is implicitly a bet that the future looks like the
  past.
- **Sub-optimization.** When a subsystem pursues its own goal so hard it undermines the
  larger system's purpose — a team hitting its local metric while the product suffers, a
  service protecting its own latency by dumping load on a neighbor. Hierarchy *causes* this
  risk: too much central control smothers the subsystems, but too much subsystem autonomy
  fragments the whole. Healthy hierarchy balances the two.
