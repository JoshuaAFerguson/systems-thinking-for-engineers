# Capstone — Analysis

> **Your work.** This is the capstone deliverable: a full systems analysis of one real system
> you know well. Work the sections in order — each builds on the last. Bring in tools from
> across the curriculum (behavior-over-time graphs, stocks and flows, CLDs, delays,
> archetypes, leverage points) and, above all, connect **structure** to **behavior**. Link
> images or model files where useful. Full references are in
> [reading-list.md](../../resources/reading-list.md).

## The system

_Name the one real system you're analyzing and describe it in a few sentences. State where
you drew the system boundary — and why there. What did you deliberately leave outside it?_

## 1. The problem as behavior-over-time

_Frame the problem as a pattern over time, not an event. Sketch the reference behavior graph:
what has the key variable actually done over months/years, and what did you want it to do?
Name the gap between the two curves._

## 2. Structure — CLD and/or stock-and-flow

_Draw the structure that produces that behavior. Include a causal loop diagram (label each
loop reinforcing/balancing and name it) and/or a stock-and-flow model where accumulations
matter. Mark the delays. Link the diagram or model file._

## 3. System archetype(s)

_Does the structure match a known archetype (Fixes that Fail, Shifting the Burden, Limits to
Growth, Tragedy of the Commons, Success to the Successful, Eroding Goals, …)? Name it and show
how your loops map onto it. If none fits cleanly, say so and explain why._

## 4. Leverage-point recommendations, ranked

parameters toward structure, information flows, rules, goals, paradigms. For each: what it
changes, roughly how hard it is, and why it sits where it does in the ranking._

## 5. Narrative — structure to behavior

_Tie it together in prose. Explain how the structure in §2 produces the behavior in §1, and
therefore why the leverage points in §4 are ranked as they are. This is the section that
proves you understand the system, not just diagrammed it._

## Assumptions, boundaries, and uncertainty

_State your key assumptions, where the boundary might be wrong, what you left out, and which
parts of the analysis are speculative versus observed. Be explicit — moving the boundary
changes the answer._
