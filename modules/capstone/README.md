# Capstone — A Full Systems Analysis

**Capstone**

This is where the whole curriculum converges. You've built up a toolkit across four phases —
the system lens, stocks and flows, feedback loops, delays, causal loop diagrams, resilience,
archetypes, leverage points, and the sociotechnical applications of the last phase. The
capstone asks you to pick **one real system you know well** and run the full analysis on it,
end to end, connecting *structure* to *behavior* the way every module has insisted you can.

No new theory here. This is the exam you write for yourself, in public.

## The brief

Choose a single real system you have genuine, first-hand knowledge of — a service, a team, a
delivery pipeline, a product's growth engine, an on-call rotation, an open-source project's
contributor flow. Pick something whose *behavior over time* has puzzled or frustrated you.
The best capstone subjects are ones where the obvious "fix" hasn't worked — that's usually a
sign the leverage is somewhere non-obvious.

Then produce a written systems analysis that a peer could read and *act on*.

## Deliverables

Work them in this order; each builds on the last. Capture your work in
[analysis.md](analysis.md).

- [ ] **1. The problem as behavior-over-time.** Frame the problem not as an event or a
      complaint but as a **pattern of behavior over time** — the reference behavior graph.
      What has the key variable actually done over months or years, and what did you *want* it
      to do? (Phase 1 — the system lens; Module 01.)
- [ ] **2. A causal loop diagram and/or a stock-and-flow model.** Draw the structure. Use a
      **CLD** to show the feedback loops (Module 03), and/or a **stock-and-flow** model where
      accumulations and rates matter (Module 02). Mark the **delays** (Module 04). Name each
      loop (reinforcing / balancing) and what it does.
- [ ] **3. System archetype(s) identified.** Does the structure match a known **archetype**
      (Module 07) — Fixes that Fail, Shifting the Burden, Limits to Growth, Tragedy of the
      Commons, Success to the Successful, Eroding Goals? Name it and show how your loops map
      onto it. If none fits cleanly, say so and say why.
- [ ] **4. Leverage-point recommendations, ranked.** Propose interventions and **rank them by
      effectiveness** using Meadows' leverage ladder (Module 08). Push past parameter tweaks
      toward structure, information flows, rules, goals, and paradigms. For each, say what it
      changes and roughly how hard it is.
- [ ] **5. A written narrative connecting structure to behavior.** Tie it together in prose:
      *this structure produces that behavior; therefore these leverage points, in this order.*
      This is the piece that proves you understand the system rather than just diagrammed it.

## What good looks like

A strong capstone clears this quality bar:

- **The structure explains the observed behavior.** Someone reading your CLD/model can see
  *why* the graph in deliverable 1 looks the way it does. The diagram isn't decoration — it's
  the explanation. If the structure and the behavior don't line up, the model is wrong or
  incomplete; fix it.
- **Recommendations target real leverage, not just parameters.** At least some of your
  interventions act on structure, feedback, rules, or goals — not only on knob-turning.
  You've explicitly ranked them and justified the ranking (Module 08).
- **Assumptions and boundaries are explicit.** You state where you drew the system boundary
  and why, what you left out, and what you're unsure about. (Module 00: moving the boundary
  changes the answer.) You distinguish what you *observed* from what you *inferred*.
- **Delays and non-linearities are accounted for.** You've marked where consequences arrive
  late and where response is non-proportional — the things that break linear reasoning.
- **It's honest.** Where the analysis is speculative, it says so. Where a favored fix is low
  leverage, it admits it. Learning in public means showing the seams.

## How to work it

1. Read the brief and the deliverables. Pick your system.
2. Draft each deliverable in order in [analysis.md](analysis.md) — the structure is already
   scaffolded there.
3. Pressure-test against the quality bar above.
4. Answer the [self-check](self-check.md) honestly.
5. Tick the capstone off in the [progress tracker](../../README.md#progress).

Reach back into any module as you go. Full references are in
[reading-list.md](../../resources/reading-list.md); Meadows' *Thinking in Systems* and her
"Leverage Points" essay are the two you'll lean on most.
