# Module 06 — Mental Models

**Phase 1 · Foundations**

Every system you've mapped so far, you mapped through a lens: your own mental model. This
module turns the tools on the observer. Mental models decide what you even *notice*, and
Argyris' **Ladder of Inference** shows how you leap from raw data to confident conclusions
without seeing a single rung. This is the foundation's capstone — the discipline that keeps
all the others honest.

## Objectives

By the end of this module you should be able to:

1. Explain what a **mental model** is and how it *filters* perception — why two people looking
   at the same system see different systems.
2. Walk the **Ladder of Inference** rung by rung: observable data → selected data → added
   meaning → assumptions → conclusions → beliefs → action.
3. Distinguish **advocacy** from **inquiry** and use the **"left-hand column"** to surface
   what you think but don't say.
4. Hold the stance that **all models are incomplete** — useful, testable, and always revisable.

## Key concepts

- **Mental models filter perception.** A mental model is the internal picture — assumptions,
  generalizations, images — through which you interpret the world. It's not a passive lens;
  it actively *selects* what you notice and discards the rest. In Senge's framing, working
  with mental models is a core discipline of a learning organization precisely because these
  hidden pictures govern action, often without our awareness.
- **The Ladder of Inference (Argyris).** A metaphor for how fast we climb from reality to
  action. From the bottom: (1) **observable data** — everything a camera would record; (2) we
  **select** a slice of it; (3) add **meanings** (cultural and personal); (4) make
  **assumptions** on those meanings; (5) draw **conclusions**; (6) adopt **beliefs** about the
  world; (7) take **action**. We climb in a blink and then treat the top rung as obvious fact.
- **The reflexive loop.** Our beliefs at the top feed back to bias which data we even select
  at the bottom — so the ladder is self-confirming. We see what we already believe, which
  reinforces the belief. This is why disagreements harden.
- **Advocacy vs. inquiry.** Advocacy is stating and defending your view; inquiry is genuinely
  asking after others' data and reasoning. Skilled dialogue balances the two — making your own
  ladder visible ("here's the data I'm reacting to and how I read it") *and* asking others to
  walk down theirs.
- **The left-hand column.** An exercise (from Argyris via Senge's *Fieldbook*): write a real
  conversation on the right, and in a left-hand column write what you were thinking and
  feeling but didn't say. The gap between the two columns is where your untested assumptions
  and defensive reasoning live.
- **All models are incomplete.** "The map is not the territory." Every model — including every
  loop diagram in this curriculum — leaves things out. That's fine; the goal isn't a perfect
  model but a *useful, testable* one you keep revising as reality pushes back.
